Our Mission

The South Side Healthy Community Organization (SSHCO) is committed to improving health outcomes and reducing health disparities for residents of Chicago's South Side communities. Through collaborative partnerships with healthcare providers, community organizations, and residents, we work to address social determinants of health, expand access to quality healthcare services, and build a healthier, more equitable future for all South Side residents.

Understanding the Hardship Index

What is the Hardship Index?

The Hardship Index is a standardized measure (0-100 scale) that quantifies socioeconomic conditions in communities by analyzing multiple demographic and economic indicators. Developed by sociologists and used by policymakers, it provides a comprehensive assessment of community well-being, with higher scores indicating greater economic distress and social challenges.

How is it Measured?

The index combines six key socioeconomic indicators, each weighted equally and standardized to create a composite score:

Economic Factors:
  • • Unemployment rate (% adults without work)
  • • Per capita income levels
  • • Poverty rate (% below federal poverty line)
Social Factors:
  • • Educational attainment (% without high school diploma)
  • • Housing quality and overcrowding rates
  • • Access to transportation and services
Score Interpretation:
  • 0-25: Low hardship (excellent conditions)
  • 26-50: Moderate hardship
  • 51-75: High hardship
  • 76-100: Extreme hardship (crisis conditions)
Health Impact Correlation:
  • • Every 10-point increase correlates with 2-3 years reduced life expectancy
  • • Higher chronic disease rates (diabetes, hypertension)
  • • Increased emergency room utilization
  • • Poor birth outcomes and maternal health
  • • Limited healthcare access and preventive care
  • • Mental health and substance abuse challenges

Critical Finding: SSHCO communities score 67% higher than the national average and 52% higher than Chicago's average, indicating severe economic hardship that directly correlates with poor health outcomes and reduced life expectancy. This data demonstrates the urgent need for comprehensive social and health interventions.
